Edison Amberola 30

Internal-horn cylinder phonograph that plays 4-minute Blue Amberol records

MakerThomas A. Edison, Inc. — Orange, New Jersey
ModelAmberola 30 (smallest of the 30 / 50 / 75 series)
TypeInternal-horn cylinder phonograph; 4-minute Blue Amberol
Approx. date1915–1929 (introduced 1915)
CaseOak, tabletop, with domed lid
ReproducerDiamond C (long-neck) — confirm from machine
ConditionAppears complete; runs (shown on video)

The Amberola 30 was the smallest and least expensive of Edison’s post-1915 “price-named” internal-horn cylinder machines (the 30 / 50 / 75 series). It is a straight 4-minute machine that plays Blue Amberol celluloid cylinders — the most durable and most commonly found cylinder type, made through the end of Edison phonograph production in 1929.

Because the horn is internal, there is no external horn to be “missing” — a complete, running Amberola 30 is a usable, compact tabletop machine. This example appears complete with its reproducer, and a short video shows it running.

Highlights

  • Plays Blue Amberol cylinders — the most available cylinder type, so a buyer can actually use it.
  • Compact oak tabletop machine; internal horn means nothing external to lose or damage.
  • Appears complete with reproducer; a running video is available.
  • Including a group of cylinders makes it a ready-to-play package.

Edison Home Phonograph — black morning-glory horn

Early banner-decal Edison Home with a black morning-glory horn on a crane

MakerThomas A. Edison — Orange, New Jersey
ModelHome, Model A or early B (banner decal)
TypeExternal-horn cylinder phonograph; 2-minute
Approx. datec. 1905–1906 (serial ~169243; banner-decal era)
CaseOak, coffin-top, with gold striping
HornBlack morning-glory (petalled) horn on a crane
ReproducerModel C (2-minute)
PlaysYes — runs and plays well (shown on video)

The Edison Home was the best-selling Edison cylinder machine. This one carries the early “EDISON HOME PHONOGRAPH” banner decal, which Edison used on the Model A and early Model B; the banner gave way to a small script decal around serial 212,000, so this machine’s serial (~169,243) places it comfortably in the banner-decal era, around 1905–1906.

It is shown as a complete system — machine, black morning-glory horn, crane, and a group of 2-minute cylinders. A black morning-glory horn is the look most buyers want; it sits a tier above a plain conical “witch’s-hat” horn and below a hand-painted flowered horn.

Of the two Edison Homes in this collection, this is the strong player — it runs and plays well, as the video shows. A machine that actually plays is markedly easier to sell and worth more than one that needs work.

Edison Home Phonograph — hand-painted rose horn ★

All-original, working Edison Home with a large hand-painted morning-glory horn

MakerThomas A. Edison — Orange, New Jersey
ModelHome, Model A or early B (banner decal)
TypeExternal-horn cylinder phonograph; 2-minute
Approx. datec. 1903–1915
CaseOak
HornLarge HAND-PAINTED morning-glory horn (cream w/ pink roses), likely Tea Tray Co.
ReproducerModel C (2-minute)
ConditionAll-original; runs but does NOT play properly as-is — needs servicing

This is the most eye-catching piece in the collection — an all-original Edison Home whose standout feature is the large hand-painted morning-glory (“flower”) horn, cream/yellow with pink roses, almost certainly an aftermarket Tea Tray Company horn of the period. Mechanically it is honest: it runs but does not play correctly as-is and needs servicing/adjustment (see the video), so it is best sold as a restoration or display piece — or serviced to play.

Edison almost never shipped hand-painted flower horns from the factory, and because they were painted with water-soluble dye on bare tin, very few survive bright and intact. A clean, colorful original flower horn is genuinely scarce and is what makes this machine far more desirable — and more saleable — than a comparable machine with a plain horn.

Highlights

  • All-original with a rare hand-painted horn — exactly what collectors chase (sold as-is; needs service to play correctly).
  • Large hand-painted rose / morning-glory horn: the photogenic, emotional draw of the piece.
  • Period-correct early banner-decal Home paired with a decorated horn = a complete parlor story.
  • A small horn can also be fitted to the reproducer directly when the large horn isn’t displayed.

Edison Standard Phonograph (project)

Oak Edison Standard with domed lid — a non-working restoration project

MakerThomas A. Edison — Orange, New Jersey
ModelStandard, Model A “New Style” (1901) or Model B (~1904–1909)
TypeExternal-horn cylinder phonograph
Approx. datec. 1901–1909
CaseOak, with domed lid
ConditionNON-WORKING • no reproducer • mandrel bearing needs “boring”

The Standard was Edison’s entry-level cylinder machine and the most-collected starter model. The domed-lid oak case points to the post-1901 redesign (Model A “New Style” or the Model B “Tall” Standard).

This example is offered honestly as a project: the owner notes it is non-working, has no reproducer, and needs its mandrel bearing re-bored (a common pot-metal failure on these machines). None of these problems is fatal — parts and reproducers are reproduced and available — but together they make it a restorer’s machine rather than a ready player.

Highlights

  • Complete oak case with original finish and decal has decorator and parts value even as-is.
  • Standards have a steady pool of project buyers / restorers.
  • Honest, fully-disclosed condition — restorers prefer untouched, un-refinished machines.
  • If the motor only needs cleaning rather than repair, that improves the sale.

Columbia Graphophone “Type AT” (no. 300282)

Early oak Columbia cylinder Graphophone with an external brass flower horn

MakerAmerican Graphophone Co. (Columbia) — New York
ModelType AT
Serial300282
TypeFront-mount cylinder Graphophone; 2-minute wax
Approx. datec. 1898–1900 (an early Columbia model)
CaseOak, with corner pillars and a banner decal
HornExternal brass flower / “witch’s-hat” horn
ConditionOwner: “restoration project”

The Type AT is a genuine early Columbia / American Graphophone Co. cylinder model — a 2-minute, front-mount machine in an ornate oak case with corner pillars and a large banner decal. It is a step above Columbia’s cheap open-works “Eagle”/Q machines, though below the deluxe mahogany models. (Columbia cylinder serial numbers are not reliably dateable, so the date is given as an era.)

This example includes a period-appropriate external brass flower horn — a real plus, since many survivors are missing their horns entirely. The owner describes it as a restoration project. The key value question on any AT is condition of the pot-metal upperworks, which on this model are prone to swelling and seizing.

Highlights

  • Early (c. 1898) Columbia cylinder machine — predates the common Type A.
  • Complete display package: includes the desirable external brass flower horn.
  • Attractive oak case with an intact gold banner decal — strong visual appeal.
  • Plays standard 2-minute wax cylinders (same as a 2-minute Edison) once serviced.

Columbia Graphophone — mahogany (likely Type BG “Sovereign”) ★

Deluxe mahogany Columbia cylinder machine — the most valuable of the group

MakerAmerican Graphophone Co. (Columbia) — New York
ModelLikely Type BG “Sovereign” (to be confirmed)
TypeCylinder Graphophone; long mandrel (2-minute + 3-minute “Twentieth Century”)
Approx. datec. 1906–1909
CaseSolid red mahogany, gold pinstriping, corner columns
MotorFour-spring (quadruple) motor
Mark“Grand Prize, Paris Exposition 1900” medallion

This is the prize of the collection. A red-mahogany, gold-pinstriped Columbia cylinder machine of this grade is almost certainly the deluxe Type BG, marketed as the “Sovereign” (introduced 1906). Mahogany was used on only a few upper-tier Columbia models, catering to affluent buyers; the BG featured a four-spring motor and a long mandrel that plays both standard 2-minute cylinders and Columbia’s 6-inch “Twentieth Century” 3-minute cylinders.

The “Grand Prize, Paris Exposition 1900” medallion is a Columbia marketing mark used across many machines, so the model is identified by the mahogany case and mechanism rather than by the medallion. Final confirmation (BG vs. the similar BM or the taller BC “Twentieth Century”) comes from the bedplate model letters, the reproducer type, and the mandrel length.

The mahogany Columbia

Highlights

  • The valuable one — roughly 2–4× an oak Type AT; lead with this.
  • Deluxe solid-mahogany cabinet with gold pinstriping photographs as “high-end antique.”
  • Four-spring motor and long mandrel — plays both 2-minute and 3-minute cylinders.
  • Genuinely scarcer than the oak machines (mahogany was a low-volume premium option).
  • Intact decals and Paris-1900 medallion support a premium — emphasize originality.

Cheney Phonograph — upright console

Collectible Chicago-made upright — a step above a common Victrola

MakerThe Cheney Talking Machine Co. — Chicago
ModelUpright console, higher-trim style (no model plates were used)
TypeInternal-horn lateral 78 rpm disc phonograph
Approx. datec. 1917–1925
CaseMahogany, carved fretwork grille, lower record-storage doors, Queen Anne feet
Tone armDistinctive gold-plated cast “stepped” tone arm

Cheney was founded in 1914 by concert violinist Forest Cheney, who designed his machines around an acoustic theory rather than copying Victor: sound was routed through a stepped, folded tone arm into resonating wood chambers and a violin-shaped internal horn. The design was distinctive enough that Cheney beat Victor in a 1921 patent-infringement appeal, and a Cheney won a gold medal at the 1915 Panama-Pacific Exposition. Among collectors, Cheney is one of the more desirable “off-brands” — a notch above generic Victrola clones.

This is an upright console floor model in mahogany with a carved fretwork grille, a gold-plated cast tone arm, and lower record-storage doors. Cheney did not use model or serial plates, so it is identified by cabinet style as a higher-trim upright of roughly 1917–1925.

The Cheney upright console

Highlights

  • “A step above a Victrola” — a named, collectible brand designed by a concert violinist.
  • Real story: beat Victor in court (1921); gold medal at the 1915 Panama-Pacific Exposition.
  • Distinctive gold-plated stepped tone arm and carved cabinet read as fine furniture.
  • Includes lower record storage; note whether both reproducers (lateral + vertical) are present.
  • If it plays after servicing, a short video roughly doubles buyer confidence.

Wooden cylinder-record storage box (empty)

Antique peg-style wooden case built to hold cylinder records — sold empty

What it isAntique cylinder-record storage / carrying case
ConstructionWood with a coated-paper covering; metal corner guards, latches & handle
HoldsRows of numbered pegs for standard 4¼-inch cylinders (~30+ capacity)
ContentsEMPTY — no cylinders included
Approx. datec. 1900s–1910s
ConditionHonest age wear to the covering; pegs intact

An antique wooden storage and carrying case made to hold cylinder records. Inside are rows of numbered wooden pegs that each cylinder slips over, and the lid is felt-padded to cushion the cylinder tops. The body is wood with the usual period coated-paper covering, protected by metal corner guards, with latches and a side carrying handle.

It is sold EMPTY — there are no cylinders included; the pegs are simply the storage fittings. These peg cases were the standard period way to store and carry a cylinder collection, so it pairs naturally with any of the cylinder machines above, or with a collection a buyer already owns.

Highlights

  • Period-correct peg storage for standard 2- and 4-minute cylinders — a useful accessory for any cylinder-machine owner.
  • Complete with metal corner guards, latches and a carrying handle.
  • Sold honestly as empty — no cylinders — so there are no condition surprises.
  • A natural add-on sale alongside one of the Edison or Columbia cylinder machines.
